# Computation vs Biology and Foundations vs Frontiers

## Quick Difference

| Axis | One side | Other side | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Computation vs biology | Algorithms, inference, representation, optimization | Genes, cells, proteins, variants, disease, drugs | Methods must answer real biological questions |
| Foundations vs frontiers | Durable principles and classic models | New foundation models and current research | Students need both stability and modern context |

## Detailed Explanation
Lecture 1 defines the course around two balancing acts. The first is computation versus biology: students need enough algorithmic depth to understand why models work, and enough biological depth to know what the model output means. The second is foundations versus frontiers: students learn classic tools such as [[expectation-maximization]] and [[hidden-markov-models]], while also studying [[representation-learning]], [[graph-neural-networks]], and generative AI.

## When the Computation Side Dominates
The risk is that the model becomes technically impressive but biologically ungrounded. A good score is not automatically a mechanism.

## When the Biology Side Dominates
The risk is that the explanation remains qualitative and cannot scale to high-dimensional data. Computation helps make large biological systems searchable, testable, and comparable.

## Biological Example
The lecture frames DNA, RNA, proteins, molecules, and disease systems as biological objects that become computable only after careful representation.

## ML Interpretation
The method is never separate from the representation. A model's assumptions determine which biological patterns it can see and which it may miss.
